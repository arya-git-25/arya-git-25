Banking & Financial Services
India's largest sector by market cap — always leads recovery after every crisis

Why banking falls in every crisis

Banks are the first victims of every crisis because fear of Non-Performing Assets (NPAs) spikes, credit growth halts, and FII (foreign institutional) investors flee. In 2008, global liquidity dried up. In 2018, the IL&FS/NBFC collapse froze lending. In 2020, COVID moratoriums threatened mass defaults. In 2024–25, RBI's tightening and FII outflows (₹1.6 lakh crore exited in FY26) hammered valuations. Yet Indian banks have recovered powerfully every single time because India's credit-to-GDP ratio remains low (~55%) vs. global peers, giving banks a massive runway for future growth.

Information Technology
India's global export engine — US/Europe spending drives 70–80% of revenues

Why IT falls in crisis

Indian IT is almost entirely dependent on US and European enterprise spending. When Western economies slow or fear recession, Indian IT companies see deal slowdowns, project cancellations, and visa cost increases. The 2000–01 dot-com bust was devastating for IT. In 2008, BFSI clients (banks) — the biggest IT spenders — froze budgets. In 2022–23, US Fed rate hikes triggered massive layoffs in US tech, killing discretionary IT spending. In 2024–25, AI automation concerns raised fears of commoditization of Indian IT services. Every single time, Indian IT has recovered and grown stronger.

FMCG stocks rarely crash due to business problems — they fall due to valuation correction and portfolio rebalancing. FMCG stocks are never "cheap" on P/E — their premium is justified by brand moats, consistent dividends, and predictable cash flows. But corrections create entry windows.

Metals & Mining
Highly cyclical — crashes deep, recovers powerfully with global capex supercycle

Why metals fall in crisis

Metals are the most cyclical sector — prices depend entirely on global demand (China drives 50% of world metals demand). In 2008, steel prices crashed 70%, aluminium 50%. In 2015–16, China's slowdown fear crushed metals globally. In 2020, construction halted globally. In 2022–23, China's real estate crisis caused iron ore and steel prices to collapse. Indian metal companies also suffer from high debt (steel plants are capital-intensive) making them vulnerable to rate hikes. But every single metals cycle ends with a powerful recovery — India's infrastructure supercycle makes this sector crucial.
